Project 3: Informative Video

Artist's Statement

Whether I like to admit it or not, I go to McDonald’s on a frequent basis, and I am consistently in awe of how much money the restaurant is able to get out of me on each visit. Nevertheless, there are definite ways I have learned to save money in the process. I wanted to make a video to share my knowledge, and even if the information is something you already know, I would like to entertain.

Initially I sought to make a video of mainly in-person footage: outside and inside McDonalds, I planned to read a script in front of the camera. During the production process, my vision changed, and I instead pivoted to a full voiceover video, substituting my own B-roll with footage, audio, and pictures in the Creative Commons. I did this namely for convenience’s sake – proposing video projects with lofty goals is something I am too good at. In spite of restructuring, I believe sound and video was used effectively in my work. I used sound effects to gauge audience direction, and video to provide background and humor.

Premiere Pro is a program I am well accustomed to, and as such, I was able to use my creative voice in a confident manner. With easygoing background music and a slightly sardonic voiceover, I created a video in homage to commentary videos from early YouTube. The intricately timed edits and audio mixing was something I would not have been able to implement in a simpler program such as iMovie or Windows Movie Maker. Any limitations of Premiere were ones I have been accustomed to – and at this point, I believe my own workflow to be my greatest hinderance. Streamlining the asset collection process, to even programming my own hotkeys and macros are examples of ideas I can implement to make the editing process work frictionless.

Overall, I am fond of my final product. I would not say that it is my best work – there are assuredly areas I could flesh out or present in a more creative manner had I more time in both the video itself and in the creative process. My voiceover, though communicated effectively, is held back by my headphone’s poor microphone quality. And ultimately, I believe the editing and charisma are carrying the brunt of my video’s effectiveness: the information I present is nothing groundbreaking or enlightening to anyone with some knowledge of McDonalds.

I am proud of my ability to keep my assets to items available in Creative Commons. I undeniably had some exceptions to this rule, but overall, my ability to keep B-roll and images in the realm of public domain is something I want to continue to work on.

Keeping assets in the public domain was not easy, however, especially in commentating on a company like McDonalds. I had to use vintage and foreign advertisements in video, and often times I was unable to convey the emotion I wanted to in an image through what I had available on Wikimedia Commons or Openverse.

At the end of the day, while my video How to Game McDonalds may not have been the final product I envisioned, and though it is undeniably held back in some areas such as audio quality and informative content, the editing style, humor, and visual graphics come together to create a video I am proud to call my own. The experience I gained in navigating public domain sources, alongside refining my production process between Premiere, Audition, and Photoshop are accomplishments that will prove valuable for my future endeavors.
